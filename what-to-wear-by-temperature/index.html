Start here

Use apparent temperature, not the headline number

The displayed air temperature is only one part of comfort. Wind can strip warmth from exposed skin, humidity can make heat linger, and direct sun can make a mild reading feel warmer. That is why OZSNAP starts with apparent, or “feels-like”, temperature.

Use the scale as a base layer decision. Then check the day’s maximum and minimum. A 17°C afternoon after a 7°C morning needs a removable layer even though the middle of the day looks mild.

Personal comfort still wins.People who run cold can move one level warmer. Walking or active days often need one level lighter than standing still for the same temperature.

The complete scale

OZSNAP’s 10 outfit levels

Levels run from warmer clothing at level 1 to the lightest heat-ready clothing at level 10. The boundaries are deliberately narrow around cool and mild weather, where one extra layer makes the biggest practical difference.

≤5°
Level 1 · Deep cold

Warm coat and full layers

Coat, knit, warm trousers and closed shoes. Add scarf or beanie for exposed starts.

6–9°
Level 2 · Cold

Coat over a warm knit

A medium coat and warm socks usually cover city plans without heavy accessories.

10–12°
Level 3 · Layered

Jumper plus light jacket

Two removable layers handle a cold start and a more comfortable afternoon.

13–15°
Level 4 · Cool

One light jumper

Long trousers and closed shoes are enough. Skip the coat, scarf and beanie.

16–18°
Level 5 · Light layer

Tee plus overshirt

Keep the outer layer easy to remove in sunshine or warm indoor spaces.

19–21°
Level 6 · Mild

Light long sleeves

Light trousers and a breathable top work without an additional jacket.

22–24°
Level 7 · Warm

Tee and light trousers

Choose airy fabric and comfortable shoes. An outer layer is rarely useful.

25–27°
Level 8 · Summer

Tee and shorts

Breathable basics suit a warm day; add sun protection around midday.

28–31°
Level 9 · Hot

Loose summer kit

Prioritise airflow, a cap, sunglasses, sunscreen and reliable shade.

≥32°
Level 10 · Extreme heat

Maximum airflow and shade

Loose linen, a broad hat and water. Reduce direct afternoon sun where possible.

Change one thing

Rain, wind and UV do different jobs

Rain

Protect the outer layer

Keep the temperature-based base outfit. Add a compact waterproof shell and shoes that tolerate wet ground rather than making every layer heavier.

Wind

Block moving air

At exposed lookouts and coastal edges, a thin wind-resistant layer can outperform a warmer but open knit.

UV

Treat it separately

UV is not a heat scale. Protection can be useful from UV index 3 even on a cool or cloudy day.

Day range

Make layers removable

A large morning-to-afternoon change calls for two light layers instead of one bulky item that only works for part of the day.

Same number, different day

Adjust the level for where you will actually be

A feels-like temperature is the best starting point, but the setting decides which layer earns a place in your bag. Think about exposure, shade, humidity and how quickly the temperature will change rather than dressing for a city-wide label.

Tropical north

Prioritise airflow and quick drying

In Cairns or Darwin, humidity can make a warm day feel persistent after sunset. Loose, breathable clothes and a compact rain layer are usually more useful than a thick jumper.

Southern coast

Carry a wind-blocking layer

Melbourne, Hobart and exposed Sydney edges can cool quickly when the wind changes. A light shell over the base outfit often works better than adding a bulky knit.

Dry inland air

Plan for the daily range

Canberra and inland trips can move from a cold morning to a comfortable afternoon. Wear two light layers that can be removed and carried without becoming a burden.

Beach and headland

Expect more exposure

Gold Coast and Perth coastal plans may feel windier than nearby streets. Secure hats, choose a layer that closes at the front and keep dry clothing for after the water.

Build a small useful kit

Choose fabric by the job it needs to do

For hot or humid weather, lightweight linen and technical quick-dry fabrics release heat and recover faster after a shower. Cotton is comfortable for ordinary dry days, but a soaked cotton layer can stay cold against the skin. In cool weather, a light wool or fleece mid-layer adds warmth without requiring a heavy coat.

A compact day bag does not need a second outfit. Pack the one adjustment the forecast suggests: a wind shell for an exposed lookout, a waterproof outer for showers, a warm layer for a late finish or sun protection for a high-UV day.

  1. Dress for the coldest fixed part of the day. Waiting at a station or watching sport feels cooler than walking.
  2. Make the first extra layer removable. It should fit in your bag once the temperature rises.
  3. Keep rain protection outside the insulation. A shell protects the useful warmth underneath.
  4. Check UV separately. A cool temperature does not mean low UV exposure.

Quick answers

Common what-to-wear questions

Should I dress for the maximum or the feels-like temperature?

Use the feels-like temperature for the time you will be outside. If your plans span the day, check morning, afternoon and evening separately and make the difference removable.

When should I move one level warmer?

Move warmer when you run cold, will be standing still, are heading to an exposed or shaded place, or expect to stay out after sunset. Move lighter for sustained walking or active work.

Does cloudy weather mean I need warmer clothing?

Not automatically. Cloud can reduce direct solar warmth, but wind, humidity and apparent temperature are better guides. Keep UV protection in mind because cloud does not remove UV risk.
